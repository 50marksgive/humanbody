Brain

The brain is the command center of the nervous system. It is divided into various regions, each responsible for specific functions such as thinking, movement, memory, emotions, and coordination. Protected by the skull, the brain connects with the body through the spinal cord.

It processes sensory information from the environment, stores memories, controls voluntary actions, and ensures survival by regulating involuntary activities like heartbeat and breathing. The brain is highly complex and is estimated to contain over 100 billion neurons.

  • Cerebrum: Responsible for voluntary activities, intelligence, memory, and learning.
  • Cerebellum: Controls coordination, posture, and balance.
  • Midbrain: Relays sensory information and controls reflexes related to sight and sound.
  • Pons Varolii: Acts as a bridge connecting different parts of the brain and assists in breathing regulation.
  • Medulla Oblongata: Controls vital functions like breathing, heart rate, and digestion.

Spinal Cord

The spinal cord is a long, tube-like structure that runs from the brainstem down the vertebral column. It serves as the primary communication channel between the brain and the rest of the body. It is protected by vertebrae and cushioned by cerebrospinal fluid.

It is composed of nerve cells that transmit messages between the brain and muscles, glands, and organs. The spinal cord also manages reflexes, which are quick, automatic responses to stimuli that do not require brain input, like pulling your hand away from a hot object.

  • Structure: Composed of gray and white matter, encased in protective bones and membranes.
  • Function: Facilitates motor and sensory signal transmission, and governs reflex arcs.
  • Divisions: Cervical, Thoracic, Lumbar, Sacral, and Coccygeal regions.
